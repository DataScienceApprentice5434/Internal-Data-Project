# Internal-Data-Project
A project using internal data (anonymised) for my apprenticeship

# Executive Summary
The Scholarship Report is an annual report produced by BPP to provide insight into the current qualifications and activities performed by tutors within the schools in order to maintain their status as a University within the United Kingdom. The impact of this report is twofold. It allows BPP to maintain their university status and qualifications to offer and teach degrees, which is central to the operation of the business and without this would be unable to operate entirely. Additionally the report provides the different schools insight into their tutors’ activities and participation in events allowing them to investigate potential avenues for improvement and where the school is lacking compared to the other schools or BPP as a whole. To create this report a survey is sent out to staff asking them various questions to allow an insight into the activities they participate in and the qualifications they hold.  The results of the survey is then broken down into various tables and analysed to draw the necessary metrics and insights. The tables and visuals produced will then be used to create a report to provide to Ofsted, the schools and the key stakeholders. This report can then be used to draw actionable insights into where the business is currently in need of improvement and where it is currently exceeding. The annual report however often changes the metrics it reports on, meaning each year the process to extract and analyse the data is different and automation is not efficient. 

# Data Infrastructure and Tools
To begin with, the method of collection needed to be determined. In prior years various applications and methods had been used, ranging from Microsoft Forms to having managers make reports on their teams. However these methods were not effective as it left room open for user error, or the data it was holding was less secure than needed to comply with GDPR. For this report, SmartSurvey was trialled. SmartSurvey is a survey producing site that securely holds the data provided and allows advanced control over the collection and format of responses. This was used due to the sensitive nature of the responses, providing insights into the activities and qualifications of staff members and the need for anonymity as the report was not to single out staff not performing as expected but rather provide an overview of the status of each of the schools. The limits to SmartSurvey however was the rigidity of how the questions could be laid out along with the inability to maintain a live export and work on producing the report prior to the closing date as that would require a new API connection which would have taken significant work and time to implement. After the survey was developed, the next decision was what software would be used to transform and analyse the data. Excel was elected to be used due to the capabilities it has in manipulating the data to pull out the necessary insights, along with SmartSurvey providing support for transforming the results into a CSV which would allow excel to access and manipulate it. Due to the report primarily being written on Microsoft Word, Excel provided the most efficient means of creating visuals and data that could be translated into Word, as other software such as PowerBI did not offer a means of copying the visuals over to word without screenshotting the visual, reducing the quality of the image.

# Data Engineering
Once the software that was to be used was decided, the process of transforming and manipulating the data began. The initial task was drawing the necessary statistics out of the unstructured data and removing any responses that weren’t being reported on. The report only reports on the scholarly activity of staff with some teaching within their role however when the survey was distributed, many staff passed the link on to their colleagues without a teaching role. As such many responses were outside the scope of the report and needed to be removed. To do this, while maintaining data integrity by ensuring that appropriate responses were not removed, a two factor approach was taken. One of the questions on the survey asked the respondents’ roles, pairing this with sending a list of names to the managers of the schools allowed a check to be performed to remove all erroneous responses. After removing erroneous data, and clearing duplicate responses, the next step was extracting the data. The report metrics often grouped up attributes, such as “Engaged with External Providers” being if the respondents answered “Yes” to either “Attended external conference”, “wrote for an external publisher” or “attended an external event”. As such, once each KPI was determined, equations were written using excel as integrated calculations ensured that regardless of potential edits made to the data in the future, the equations would update and continue to reflect the accurate results. Determining KPIs was a time consuming task as prior reports had not sufficiently recorded the metrics used and as such during the creation of the current report, all metrics were recorded for future reference to reduce data instability over several reports. Additionally, for the first time, freelancer metrics were reported on aswell, broadening the scope of the report and including additional metrics which differed from the permanent faculty as questions needed to be worded in a different manner to be appropriate for the freelancer survey.

# Data Visualisation and Dashboards
After pulling the data and accurately producing equations to draw the necessary metrics, consideration turned to how to best visualise the data in a way that provided meaningful insight using descriptive analytics for the stakeholders while maintaining readability.  The initial proposal was a PowerBI dashboard providing visualisations of the data in a readable format. However as this was developed issues became clear, such as the number of free text responses that, while traditionally would be filtered out, were displayed on the visuals increasing visual clutter along with adding confusion. 

![Graph](https://github.com/DataScienceApprentice5434/Internal-Data-Project/blob/main/images/Graph.png)
 
This resulted in the PowerBI dashboard being discontinued, instead opting for trialling a Excel Pivot Chart approach. This however also was discontinued as flaws began to present. While Pivot Chart connected directly to the dataset, which sat in an XLSX File following transformation from a CSV to allow for Excel equations, creating multiple visualisations referring to different segments of the data was inefficient and led to confusion. As such, the approach that was most successful were simple Excel tables of the data alongside KPIs such as “in scope” staff to allow effective comparison from an initial look without the need to dissect graphs to determine the statistic required. This was especially important due to the stakeholders of the report being high level managerial positions which would not have the time to drill down and dissect graphs to pull the actionable insight they need. 

# Data Analytics
Various analytical methods and algorithms were applied to the data to extract the necessary data, such as using deductive content analysis to draw the highest qualification held by staff in scope. Leaving the data as free text meant that many responses were missed as respondents instead of choosing one of the options selected “Other” and inputted their qualification despite the qualification being one of the options or equivalent to one. As such an equation was made that converted the free text responses that contained transferable qualifications such as “I hold a level 6” into “Level 6” through searching the strings for the words. This was not completely effective as some staff may hold qualifications which are equivalents but not detected however given the timescale of the project, having two weeks to complete the analysis, it was the most effective solution that could be provided. Then data not converted to fit with the predetermined options were converted into “None / Do not hold” options. This was to ensure that any inaccuracies published would be a downplay of the statistics rather than claiming to hold higher average qualifications than is correct. This method was applied to various questions wherein an “Other” box was allowed. In future reports the necessity of free text boxes in such questions will be interrogated to determine if data would be a higher integrity without free text boxes due to the removal of additional human input. Additionally, many metrics were combinations of multiple questions and as such equations were made to combine and convert questions to look for specific values amongst columns not adjacent to each other, removing the ability for a linear search. Equations such as “=IF(EXACT(D2,"Yes"),1,IF(EXACT(E2,"Yes"),1,IF(EXACT(H2,"Yes"),1, IF(EXACT(Z2, "Yes"), 1, IF(EXACT(W2, "Yes"), 1, IF(EXACT(X2, "Yes"), 1,0))))))” were used to extract the data necessary such as whether a member of staff held a professional qualification or was studying for one. In the future, equations should be further refined to reduce system strain from running such complex and conditional equations to ensure the report is efficient with as few avenues of error as possible. This could be achieved through a List.Contains equation in PowerQuery. Overall however the equations and report drew the necessary insights to provide actionable intel to the stakeholders and allow BPP to continue operating, as such the project held a massive impact to the business, ensuring continued operation and providing insight into improvements.
